The Melatonin-Cortisol War

In a healthy biological system, Melatonin and **Cortisol** operate on a see-saw. As the sun sets, Melatonin should rise and Cortisol should drop. However, in our modern world of "Always-on" productivity and blue light, this rhythm is shattered.

When Cortisol remains high at 10:00 PM, it actively inhibits the pineal gland. You might feel "tired but wired"—your body is exhausted, but your brain is in a state of **Hyper-Vigilance**, scanning for non-existent threats. This is the primary killer of the 90-minute sleep cycle.

Stress FAQ

1. How does exercise affect Cortisol Timing? ↓

High-intensity training (HIT) late at night can spike Cortisol for 3-4 hours. For optimal sleep, finish heavy workouts at least 6 hours before your 90-minute cycle start time.

2. Can blue light trigger Cortisol? ↓

Absolutely. Blue light mimics the morning sun. When it hits your retina at night, your brain assumes it is morning and triggers a Cortisol release, killing your Melatonin production instantly.

3. What is the "Cortisol Awakening Response" (CAR)? ↓

CAR is a 35-60% increase in Cortisol levels right after waking. It is a healthy response that "boots up" your brain and prepares you for the day's demands.

4. Does fasting increase nighttime Cortisol? ↓

Extended fasting can raise Cortisol as the body looks for fuel. If you have sleep issues, a small complex-carb snack before bed can actually help keep Cortisol low by stabilizing blood sugar.

5. How long does it take to fix a broken Cortisol rhythm? ↓

With strict morning sunlight exposure and evening light blocking, most people can "re-sync" their Cortisol curve in 7 to 14 days.
